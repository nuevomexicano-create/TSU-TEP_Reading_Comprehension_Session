Time Is Your Biggest Challenge

60 minutes ÷ 40 questions = 1.5 minutes per question

But you must also read each passage — so you need a plan.

Short passage × 4~6 min each — 2 min read + 4 min questions
Long passage × 2~15 min each — 4 min read + 11 min questions
Total used24 + 30 = 54 min — leaves 6 minutes to review
Rule: Never spend more than 2 minutes on one question. Mark it, move on, come back at the end.
Checkpoint: At question 20, you should have at least 30 minutes remaining.
⏱ 0:10 – 0:22
01

Skimming

Read fast — get the big picture

What is it?

Reading very quickly to understand the general topic and structure — without reading every word.

Use it for:

  • The start of every passage (always)
  • "What is this text mainly about?"
  • "What is the purpose of this text?"
  • "What does the author discuss?"

How to skim:

  1. Read the title (if there is one)
  2. Read the first sentence of each paragraph
  3. Read the last sentence of the whole text
  4. Note any headings, bold words, or lists
  5. Time allowed: 30–60 seconds maximum
Do NOT read every sentence — you will run out of time.
⏱ 0:22 – 0:34
02

Scanning

Search fast — find specific information

What is it?

Moving your eyes quickly over the text to find a specific piece of information — a number, name, date, or keyword.

Use it for:

  • "According to the passage…" questions
  • Questions with numbers, prices, or dates
  • Questions mentioning a specific name or place

How to scan:

  1. Read the question first
  2. Pick the key word from the question
  3. Move your eyes quickly down the passage — look for that word (or a synonym)
  4. When you find it — stop and read that sentence carefully
  5. Select your answer
The answer in the text is often a paraphrase of the question — not the exact same words.
⏱ 0:34 – 0:45
03

Vocabulary in Context

Use clues from the text to guess meaning

What is it?

Understanding an unknown word by using the surrounding sentences as clues — no dictionary needed.

Question looks like:

  • "The word X is closest in meaning to…"
  • "Which word best replaces X?"
  • "What does X refer to in line…?"

4 types of context clues:

  1. Definition — sentence explains the word
    "Nematocysts, or stinging cells, fire venom…"
  2. Synonym — similar word is used nearby
    "huge and enormous waves…"
  3. Antonym — opposite word is nearby
    "unlike the safe jellyfish, the box jellyfish…"
  4. Logic — read before AND after the word
Try substituting each answer choice — which one keeps the sentence meaning intact?
⏱ 0:45 – 0:55
04

Reading for Detail

Find the exact answer — slow down and read carefully

What is it?

Carefully reading one specific section of the passage to find an exact fact, reason, or explanation.

Use it for:

  • "Which of the following is TRUE?"
  • "Which is NOT mentioned?"
  • "According to the passage, why…?"
  • "The author states that…"

Steps:

  1. Read the question carefully
  2. Scan for the key word location in the passage
  3. Read that sentence + the one before and after
  4. Match the answer to what the passage actually says
  5. Eliminate options that mix facts from different parts
Warning: Wrong options often use true facts from the passage combined in a false way.
⏱ 0:55 – 1:05
05

Making Inferences

Understand what is suggested — not directly stated

What is it?

The answer is not written in the text. You must read between the lines and draw a logical conclusion.

Use it for:

  • "It can be inferred that…"
  • "The author implies that…"
  • "What is probably true about…?"
  • "Why most likely did…?"

How to answer:

  1. Find the relevant part of the passage
  2. Ask yourself: What does this suggest?
  3. The correct answer must be supported by the text
  4. Eliminate options that are directly stated (inference = not stated)
  5. Eliminate options that go too far beyond the text
Inference ≠ common sense. It must be logically supported by something in the passage.

Passage — Part 1 of 2 (paragraphs 1–2). Continue reading on the next slide.

THE HONEY BEE — MORE THAN JUST HONEY

The honey bee is one of the most important insects on Earth. Although many people associate bees mainly with honey, their role as pollinators is far more significant. As bees travel from flower to flower collecting nectar, they transfer pollen, which allows plants to reproduce. Scientists estimate that bees are responsible for pollinating approximately one third of all the food that humans consume, including fruits, vegetables, and nuts.

A single honey bee colony may contain up to 60,000 individual bees, all working together with remarkable efficiency. Each colony is led by a queen, whose only purpose is to lay eggs. Worker bees, which are all female, are responsible for collecting food, building and maintaining the hive, and defending the colony against threats. Male bees, known as drones, exist for just one purpose — to mate with a new queen.

Passage — Part 2 of 2 (paragraphs 3–4). Now read the questions and answer.

THE HONEY BEE — MORE THAN JUST HONEY (continued)

In recent decades, bee populations worldwide have been declining at a worrying rate. A condition known as Colony Collapse Disorder has caused worker bees to disappear suddenly from their hives, leaving behind a queen and food stores but no workers. Researchers believe that the widespread use of pesticides, loss of natural habitats, and the spread of disease are all contributing to this problem.

The consequences of losing bees could be severe. Without bees to pollinate crops, food production would fall considerably and prices for many everyday foods would rise. Governments and conservation organisations are encouraging people to plant bee-friendly flowers, reduce their use of chemical pesticides, and support local beekeepers. Protecting bees ultimately means protecting our own food supply.

Test Day — 10 Essential Rules

1. Read ALL questions before reading the passage

2. Skim every passage first — always (45–60 seconds)

3. The answer is always in the text — don't use outside knowledge

4. Never leave a blank — wrong answers do not lose marks

5. Move on after 2 minutes — mark it, return later

6. Eliminate 2 wrong answers first, then choose between 2

7. For "NOT mentioned" — check every option against the text

8. Watch for distractors — true facts that don't answer the question

9. Inference must be supported by the text — not just "probably true"

10. Check the time at question 20 — you should have 30 minutes left
